thetruesize.com

Just like any map, the GeoWeb is a product of the people producing it.

Stephens (2013) conducted a survey, and found significant gender discrepancies in contributions to the OSM and Google Maps by gender. Predominantly male contributors.

Stephens (2013, p982)

The volunteers that contribute their geographic information to the GeoWeb are representing their local knowledge. This knowledge cannot be separated from the individuals that contribute it.

Stephens (2013, p982)

maps created through user-generated content are similar to their predecessors that are biased by the norms, traditions, assumptions and political biases of their map makers.

Stephens (2013, p983)

Even as the VGI supplements traditional data sources and provides a rich source of information about the material workd, when only specific demographic segments contribute information, viewpoints are unsaid, and places and people remain hidden.

Stephens (2013, p985)

Much like early promises that the Internet would universally connect infividuals without the constraints of geography, early promises of the GeoWeb envisioned cybercartography as a means for "changing power relationships and empowering previously marginialized groups" by adding themselves to the map.

Can we think of any examples?

Stephens (2013)

Looked at amenitied for users as shown on OSM. These tags allow users to mark out places on the map that may be of interest to them.

Tags must be approved by users, they need a minimum of 8 votes to become part of the map standard.

Stephens (2013, p990)

OSM users approved features to delineate between a restaurant, pub, bar, biergarten, nightclub, stripclub, swingerclub and brothel with detailed descriptions to delineate the differences.

Stephens (2013, p990)

In contrast to the brothen/swinger club/nightclub distinction, proposals for features that are feminized or 'nurturing spaces' are subject to a less distinct debate.

Stephens (2013, p990)

In April 2011 the feature "childcare" was proposed as a "place for children to do homework, play and spend time otherwise after school or kindergarten." This proposal was heavily debated and rejected 3 weeks later by a 9-5 vote.

Stephens (2013, p990)

Users opposed to the measure argued that "childcare" would be the same as the [already approved] feature "kindergarten"

Stephens (2013, p991)

On OSM spaces of care and nurture that are associated with feminized skills garner less attention than the facilities where women are commodified, and therefore do not obtain the votes necessary to become features.
